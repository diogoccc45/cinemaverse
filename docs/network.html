key metrics

The numbers.

Four metrics characterise the structure of my film network. Together they tell a consistent story: this is an unusually well-connected, highly clustered graph.

13.5
Small-World Sigma (σ)
σ = (C/C_rand) / (L/L_rand). A value above 1 indicates small-world properties. Mine is 13.5 — exceptionally high. Most real-world networks score between 1 and 5.
0.776
Clustering Coefficient (C)
The probability that two neighbours of a node are also connected to each other. 0.776 means my film clusters are extremely dense — films I watch tend to share directors and actors with each other.
3.69
Avg Path Length (L)
The average number of hops between any two films in the network. 3.69 means any two films I've watched connect through fewer than 4 steps — cinema's version of six degrees of separation.
10
Diameter
The longest shortest path in the network. Despite 509 nodes and 5,131 edges, no two films are more than 10 hops apart — a sign of a tightly connected graph.

Erdős–Rényi (random) ↗

Each pair of nodes connects with equal probability p. The result is a structureless random graph — low clustering, no hubs, Poisson degree distribution. The baseline against which all real networks are compared.
Barabási–Albert (scale-free) ↗
σ = 2.9 · C = 0.100 · L = 2.36 · m = 10
New nodes attach preferentially to already well-connected nodes — "rich get richer". Produces massive hubs (max degree 286) and a power-law degree distribution. Models the internet, citation networks and social media.
Watts–Strogatz (small-world) ↗
σ = 11.6 · C = 0.517 · L = 2.87 · k = 20 · p = 0.1
Starts as a regular ring lattice, then randomly rewires 10% of edges. The rewiring creates long-range shortcuts that dramatically reduce path length while preserving high clustering — the classic small-world recipe.

degree distribution

Is my network scale-free?

A scale-free network follows a power-law degree distribution — a straight line in log-log space. This means a small number of "hub" films connect to many others, while most films connect to few. The Barabási–Albert model is the canonical example.

What a straight line means
In log-log space, a power law P(k) ~ k^(-γ) appears as a straight line with negative slope. The Barabási–Albert model (red) shows the clearest linear trend — confirming its scale-free nature.
My network's distribution
My network (gold) doesn't follow a clean power law — it has a flatter distribution at high degrees, suggesting a mix of preferential attachment and community structure. It's not purely scale-free, but it has hubs.

community detection

Louvain communities.

The Louvain algorithm finds densely connected groups within the network by maximising modularity — a measure of how much more connected nodes within a community are compared to a random graph. Applied to my film network, it reveals the genre and director clusters that structure my taste.

Louvain identified 7 distinct communities in my network. The two largest are Drama-dominated, while smaller ones cluster around Action, Comedy and Science Fiction.

conclusion

What this says about my taste.

A strong small-world network — with a twist.
My film network has a sigma of 13.5 — 13.5 times above the small-world threshold, and higher than the Watts–Strogatz model itself. This means my taste is simultaneously highly clustered (I return to the same directors, actors and genres) and globally well-connected (any two films link in fewer than 4 steps).

The clustering coefficient of 0.776 is particularly telling: when I watch a film, the other films I've watched that share its director or lead actor are very likely to also share directors or actors with each other. My taste forms tight, coherent communities — not random exploration.

The 7 Louvain communities reflect real genre and director groupings rather than random clusters, and the degree distribution suggests hubs (blockbusters with large casts) without being fully scale-free. My network is most similar to Watts–Strogatz — the small-world model — but with higher clustering and a richer community structure.
